    3. Cross-Border Guide to Top Construction Contract Modifications
    • Dashboard
    • Comparison builder
    • Contacts

    Australia

      Contributed by:

      Lander & Rogers

      • ​Elon Zlotnick
       
      1.

      ​Insertion of limit of liability and exclusion of 'consequential and indirect loss' clauses: Parties to contracts, especially where there is an expectation of the other party only being prepared to negotiate on a few key amendments, are seeking to insert a limit of liability clause with the 'usual carve outs' (e.g. liability which by law one cannot contract out of, liability to the extent that the other party would be entitled to be indemnified for such liability under a policy of insurance required to be effected under the contract subject to the limit of insurance, liability caused by wilful misconduct or fraudulent or criminal actions, and liability for infringement of intellectual property rights) to provide certainty around the extent of their potential liability.   

       
      2.

      ​Modifications to extension of time clauses: The position to be taken on whether to push or respond to a request for tighter or looser abilities to claim an extension of time to the date by which the works are to be completed, will depend on who one is acting for. As a generalisation, we are seeing greater pushback to 'time bar' clauses which seek to prevent a party from being able to make a claim for an extension of time, should they not do so within the time frames stipulated in the contract. 

       
      3.

      ​Greater consideration being given to 'contractually assumed liability' exclusion clauses in professional indemnity insurance policies and their impact on contracts: Consultants seem to be increasingly aware of clauses within consultancy agreements which may amount to 'contractually assumed liability' and, therefore, to an insured not being covered under a standard professional indemnity insurance policy which has such an exclusion. This has led to a desire to avoid language by which one party 'warrants' or 'undertakes' to achieve an outcome, to a greater effort being spent defining the purpose for which a consultant is engaged and the metrics around achievement of that purpose.  In addition, and for the same reason, consultants’ certificates, certifying that work has been constructed in accordance with the consultant's design and deeds of novation, novating consultants to design and construct contractors by which a consultant retains some liability to its original client, are increasingly the subject of negotiation. 

       
      4.

      Achievable practical completion clauses: Parties appear to be increasingly moving towards contracts whereby some of the typical prerequisites to practical completion which contractors tend to struggle to obtain prior to practical completion (e.g. 'as built' drawings and operation and maintenance manuals), are being removed as prerequisites to practical completion and identified as work to be completed within a defined time-frame after achieving practical completion, with a developer withholding the return of the 'practical completion security' until provision of such post-practical completion documents. Tight programs and high liquidated damages rates are driving such amendments. 

       
      5.

      Tightening recourse on security and set off clauses, and increasing use of insurance bonds in place of bank guarantees: Parties are increasingly seeking to tighten up the circumstances in which a party is able to have recourse to security — given that frequently such clauses will, as a starting point, identify that recourse can be had, even if the other party is not in breach of its obligations under the contract. In addition, parties are increasingly seeking to limit the right of set off to where the other party has incurred a loss, as opposed to where it 'reasonably expects it may' suffer a loss. In addition, and as a general observation, we are increasingly seeing a willingness to accept unconditional undertakings issued by an insurer, in place of traditional bank guarantees.
